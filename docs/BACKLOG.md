## REQ-GHA-006: High-value, business-user-legible alerting (alarm treatment)
- **Goal:** A business user can act on an alert without an SRE translating it for them. Every alert that reaches a person states, in business terms, *what is degrading, for whom, and what to do next* — with the technical detail one drill-down away. This is the alarm-treatment + business-legibility layer: signal-not-noise alerting whose output is legible to non-SREs. The AI infra→business translation is the mechanism (it narrates and routes), but this requirement states the **target state**, not "use model X" or any specific translation engine.
- **Why (Igor's emphasis):** The crux of the GlassHood moat is business legibility — raw infra signals made actionable for business users, narrow-and-best, additive on top of deterministic numbers, outside the validation path. Today most alerts are SRE-framed (a raw rule identifier and a metric-equals-value line), business framing exists only for a handful of pre-enumerated anomaly metrics, and the only "AI translation" in the alert surface is actually a hardcoded `if/elif` tree. This REQ closes that gap and locks the additive-AI honesty contract that today's free-form AI score violates.
- **Scope boundary vs REQ-GHA-005 (do not re-litigate):** REQ-GHA-005 owns the **data plane** — it *produces and serves* the deterministic figures (ingest → streaming derivation of ratios/rates/percentiles → serving → BI/live-monitoring). REQ-GHA-006 owns the **alert behavior/contract plane** — how an alert is tiered, fired, severity-graded, framed, narrated, and acted on. The clean seams: (a) **derived metrics** — 005 produces the ratio/rate/percentile; 006 only requires that alerts and the alert-operating-view are *defined on* those outcome indicators (never on a raw count the view never showed); (b) **alert-record durability** — 006 owns the durability/auditability *invariant* and the alert object shape, but *where* the record is persisted is an explicit open boundary decision (see open questions), not pre-assigned to 005.

- **Target-state acceptance criteria (all written as user-observable invariants; metric names are illustrative `e.g.`, not the spec; portable across clouds):**
  - **Tiered by purpose — only broken promises demand attention.** Every alert is either a *symptom* (a stated service promise to the user was missed) or a *diagnostic* signal (reference material that helps locate a cause). Only the symptom tier is treated as attention-demanding (whatever surfaces alerts to a person); diagnostic signals are reachable on drill-down and are not treated as attention-demanding on their own. The attention-demanding tier maps one-to-one to broken promises, not to internal component noise. (A business user has their attention drawn only when something they actually care about is broken.)
  - **Fire on threatened outcomes, not raw internal thresholds.** An alert's firing condition is defined against a user-facing outcome or risk to a stated service objective — expressed as proportions/rates/distribution-tails (*e.g.* a success-rate / availability objective, an error rate, tail latency), not absolute raw counts in isolation. An alert is well-formed only if it can state, in business terms, what is degrading and for whom. *How* the objective and its budget are computed is unconstrained.
  - **Severity reflects business impact, not a static label.** "Critical" reliably means a real service promise to users is broken or imminently at risk; severity is a function of user-facing impact and the threatened objective, not pinned per-rule independent of outcome. Severity is a deterministic value. *Exception:* deterministically-justified **policy severities** are allowed (*e.g.* a compliance-driven CVE rated critical regardless of live user impact) — the invariant is that severity is grounded and trustworthy, so a business user can trust that "critical" is always justified, not that user impact is the *only* input.
  - **Self-contained and actionable, business-first.** Every alert carries a recommended next action stated in business terms (what to decide, escalate, communicate, or pause) **and** a path to deeper technical context — recipient + action travel together, so the alert is sufficient to begin responding without consulting a separate system or a human translator. The recommended action and the drill-down path are first-class attributes of the alert, not guessed ad hoc at display time. The technical runbook/detail still exists — it is reached on drill-down, never deleted. (This is the crux: a business user can escalate / pause / communicate without an SRE telling them what the alert means.)
  - **Alerts are defined on derived, outcome-level indicators — coherent across view and alerts.** A business user sees what fraction of attempts succeeded or how slow the worst-served customers are — proportions and tail experience they can calibrate — not a raw count they cannot judge (the indicator also scales with traffic and reflects proportion-of-impact). The indicators that alerts fire on and that the operating-view-for-alerts shows are outcome-level (ratio/rate/percentile, *e.g.* error rate, success proportion, tail latency), never a raw count in isolation; *how* those indicators are derived is REQ-GHA-005's, but an alert is never defined on, and never references, something the operating view has never shown.
  - **Business-outcome indicators are monitored and alertable (silent-failure catch).** The system tracks at least one business-outcome indicator — a real-world unit of value flowing through the system (*e.g.* orders / checkouts / transactions) — alongside technical indicators, and a degradation in that business value is itself an alertable condition **even when every technical signal reads healthy**. The clearest expression of the moat: the alert is already in business terms.
  - **Summary-first, drill-down for depth.** The operating view from which alerts are read and into which an alert drills down leads with service-level outcome indicators (promise-to-the-user health) with saturation and dependency signals present but subordinate (*e.g.* queue depth), offering a top-level summary in business/outcome terms by default with per-component technical depth reachable on drill-down. A business user is never forced into per-component detail to learn whether the service is healthy for users.
  - **Living, not frozen.** The alert definitions and the operating-view-for-alerts stay aligned with the system's *current* service objectives as those objectives change — what is shown and what is alerted on do not drift from the current promises to the user. The payoff: the operating picture and alerts a business user relies on stay current and trustworthy, so alerts remain meaningful to them rather than misleading them with stale indicators. This is a maintained-alignment **property**, not a fixed review cadence or governance tool.
  - **Durable, auditable alert record (enterprise-reference invariant).** Alerts and their acknowledgements form a durable, queryable record that survives restarts and is retained for an enterprise-appropriate horizon, with a full ALCOA+-grade trail of when each alert fired, was seen, and was acted on — a business-impacting alert is not lost merely because no one was watching a live view when it fired. *Durability/auditability is the invariant of THIS REQ; whether the alert record lives in a 006-owned store or is served from 005's serving layer is an open boundary decision (see open questions), not settled here. This is a deliberate enterprise-scale reference property, **not** to be value-engineered down for current data volume.*

- **Honesty constraint (the additive-AI contract — highest-risk clause, enforce STRUCTURALLY):** Every number, threshold, percentile, rate, severity, and objective an alert presents **originates in the deterministic layer**. The AI translation layer is *additive*: it explains, contextualizes, prioritizes, and composes the business-facing narrative on top of those deterministic values, binding the narrative (what happened / who is affected / what to do) to the deterministic classification and figures that justify it — into **one coherent alert** that applies generally to any alert the system can raise, not only a fixed pre-enumerated set. The AI must be *incapable* of introducing a figure or grade the deterministic layer did not produce (a structural guarantee, not a "please don't invent numbers" prompt instruction — the contract must hold even when the model misbehaves). **No AI-originated figure appears in any alert, evidence record, or report artifact as if it were a measured value.**

- **Status:** tracked, target-state (drafted 2026-06-24). Distinct from REQ-GHA-005 (data plane / pipeline / BI). Implementation deferred; sequencing TBD with Igor. **Open material decisions before implementation:** (1) where the durable alert record lives (006-owned store vs. 005's serving layer); (2) whether alert delivery/routing/escalation is in scope (alerts are pull-only today — the criteria are deliberately delivery-agnostic and do not commit to a push/notification mechanism). See open questions.

## REQ-GHA-007: One correlated incident thread (impact + change + request-path), joined by a shared identifier
- **Goal:** When any monitored component is unhealthy, a business user sees a SINGLE correlated incident thread that automatically links that component's impact signals (metrics, logs) with its change signals (what-changed drift, audit, pushed events) for the same component in the same time window — one legible "what is wrong, what changed, and why" story, with no manual cross-referencing of separate signal views. As the estate grows, the join moves from component-identity toward a propagated shared identifier (the cloud-agnostic principle of correlating signals by a shared id — e.g. a request/trace id) so one request can be followed end-to-end across services.
- **Service-map slice (the business-facing half of the request path):** Each dependency edge on the service map must carry and display its own observed call health — success/error outcome and latency — so the map highlights the failing or slow LINK between two components, not just the components (e.g. "the app→database call is slow, not the app itself").
- **Why:** Today the four-signal triage is partial — metrics + logs + events exist and already correlate, but only by node identity via the drill-in modal (node→metrics, node→logs, node→AI), plus a statistical short-window join. There is no request-path signal and no shared-identifier correlation, so the "confirm with traces" leg and the full-incident picture are missing. A business user wants the correlated OUTCOME (one plain-language thread), not the trace mechanism.
- **Acceptance:**
  - An unhealthy component yields ONE thread that, for that component and time window, surfaces its impact signals and its change signals together, readable by a non-SRE without opening separate views.
  - Each service-map dependency edge shows its own observed call outcome and latency; a slow/failing link is visually distinguishable from a slow/failing component.
  - Correlation is by a shared identifier where one is present in the source signals; component-identity is the fallback when none is.
- **State:** service map, node-scoped drill-in, topology drift feed and audit trails already exist; per-edge call health is not yet surfaced; there is no shared-identifier join.
- **Honesty constraint:** per-edge call health and the correlated thread are deterministic (collected/derived); the AI translation layer only EXPLAINS the surfaced hotspot/thread and never originates a figure or threshold. Full per-request span/subsegment/sampling/context-propagation PLUMBING is explicitly OUT of scope — it has no direct business-user payoff; only the legible correlated outcome and the per-edge hotspot are required.
- **Status:** tracked, target-state.

## REQ-GHA-008: Define "healthy" first — an SLI contract, surfaced as an SLI-led operating view
- **Goal:** For each monitored component and solution, GlassHood computes and surfaces a defined-healthy signal set — availability/success rate, latency PERCENTILES (not just averages, which hide spikes), errors expressed as a RATE, and saturation — PLUS at least one business-outcome KPI per solution, so a silent business failure (infra green, business outcome failing) is itself a visible unhealthy signal. These signals are derived deterministically (e.g. error rate = errors / total requests; availability = 1 − failures/total) and are the source-of-truth numbers that status, alerting, and the AI translation layer build on. The shared operating view then LEADS with this health contract (availability, latency, error-rate as the headline; saturation and dependency-health as early-warning), with topology and per-service deep-dive beneath it; the view is a curated, reviewable artifact whose surfaced signals can be composed per audience (business stakeholder vs operator), not a fixed unchangeable set.
- **Why:** Today the live collector pulls only a few RAW numbers (an AVERAGED load-balancer latency, request count per window, average CPU%) and the shared view leads with a topology graph plus a binary "All Systems Operational" banner and a fixed handful of tiles. A business user thus sees "infra is up" but never whether the service is keeping its availability/latency/error-rate PROMISE, and cannot catch the "tech looks green but checkouts are failing" case. Averages hide spikes; counts aren't rates; there is no business-outcome signal.
- **Acceptance:**
  - Each component/solution exposes availability, latency percentiles, error RATE, and saturation as deterministically-derived numbers.
  - At least one business-outcome KPI per solution is surfaced and can go unhealthy independently of infra health.
  - The shared operating view leads with the SLI contract (not the topology graph or a binary banner); the surfaced signal set is curatable per audience rather than fixed.
- **State:** raw latency/throughput/CPU + threshold-on-raw status exist; no percentiles, no computed error-rate/availability, no saturation SLI, no business-KPI, no derived/computed-metric capability; operating view is topology-led and tiles are fixed.
- **Honesty constraint:** SLI numbers stay deterministic source-of-truth; the additive plain-language narrative explains every deviation but never originates a figure or threshold. The HUMAN BI reporting surface and the data pipeline that feeds long-horizon analytics are REQ-GHA-005's scope and are not duplicated here — this REQ is the live SLI contract + the live operating view.
- **Status:** tracked, target-state.

## REQ-GHA-009: Promote recurring log patterns into tracked, business-legible signals
- **Goal:** A recurring log SIGNATURE in a monitored solution can be promoted into a tracked, NAMED signal that raises a business-legible alert when it spikes — so a non-SRE sees "error pattern X in service Y is rising" instead of a raw log wall. Structured log fields — including any correlation/request identifier present in the source payload — are preserved and surfaced so an incident's logs, metrics and topology can be joined for that solution (this is the log side of the REQ-GHA-007 correlation). Each monitored tenant's log RETENTION and no-sensitive-data policy is exposed as a legible, auditable statement (e.g. hot window, archive horizon, regulatory no-expiry) a non-SRE / compliance reader can trust.
- **Why:** The live log surface today reduces every entry to a minimal {timestamp, severity, message} shape — request/trace/component/user-context fields are dropped, blocking shared-ID correlation; there is no way to turn a recurring failure signature into a watched signal; and the retention + no-sensitive-data policies exist only as internal config, not as a legible per-tenant statement.
- **Acceptance:**
  - A user can promote a recurring log signature to a named signal; when that signal spikes, a business-legible alert fires naming the pattern, the service, and the direction of change.
  - Correlation/request identifiers present in the source log payload are preserved and available to join logs with metrics/topology for the same incident.
  - Each tenant's retention and no-sensitive-data policy is shown as a plain-language, auditable statement.
- **State:** real live severity-tail viewer + retention tiering config + ingest-time sensitive-data blocklist already exist; pattern-promotion, correlation-field preservation, and a surfaced policy view do not.
- **Honesty constraint:** the deterministic engine owns the promoted signal's threshold and count; the AI may explain or triage a promoted signal but never originates its threshold or count. SCOPE FENCE: "trend" here means the LIVE in-product count/rate of the named signal; long-horizon historical trending and the analytics store are REQ-GHA-005. - **Status:** tracked, target-state.

## REQ-GHA-010: Approaching-a-limit early-warning + resilient, polite reading of the monitored estate
- **Goal (two angles):**
  - **Detect-and-translate (business-facing):** GlassHood detects when a monitored component is approaching a capacity or rate limit — via headroom and early-warning indicators such as rising retries/backoff, throttle-rejection errors, or queue/latency growth — and warns the business user, in plain language with a next action, BEFORE the limit causes a degradation ("Service X is approaching a usage limit and will start degrading"), rather than the user learning of it from a downstream outage.
  - **Own read-path resilience (a business-TRUST invariant):** when reading a remote estate, GlassHood must never surface stale-or-wrong health as if it were current, and must never amplify a monitored estate's overload — both directly determine whether a business user can TRUST what GlassHood shows. Concretely: it retries only transient/throttle failures with bounded, JITTERED backoff under attempt/timeout ceilings; it breaks the circuit and self-limits its read rate when a source is throttling so it never piles onto the source's overload; and it degrades to last-known-good, CLEARLY FLAGGED as stale, rather than to wrong or silently-stale data.
- **Why:** Today no collector tracks limit headroom or throttle pressure, so there is no proactive early-warning — a business user only learns of a limit from the outage it causes. And the read path sets flat timeouts + model-failover but has no jittered backoff, no circuit-breaker, no self-rate-limit, so under a throttling source GlassHood can pile on retries and then surface stale-or-wrong health, silently misleading the business user.
- **Acceptance:**
  - An approaching capacity/rate limit in a monitored component raises an early-warning, in plain language with a next action, BEFORE it causes degradation.
  - Under a throttling/slow source, GlassHood does not increase its load on that source (bounded jittered retries on transient/throttle errors only, circuit-break + self-rate-limit), and never presents degraded data as current — stale data is always flagged stale.
- **State:** stale-cache-on-failure degradation is strong and write-path idempotency exists; jittered backoff/circuit-break/self-rate-limit and estate-side limit-pressure detection do not.
- **Honesty constraint:** all warning figures and thresholds (headroom %, the limit) remain deterministic; any AI is used only to explain them, never to originate a number. Angle (ii) has no direct business-facing UI of its own, but it is in scope as a business-TRUST invariant (it guards whether every business-facing surface can be believed), NOT as optional plumbing.
- **Status:** tracked, target-state.

## REQ-GHA-011: "Is it us or the provider?" context + stakeholder impact comms + post-incident record
- **Goal:** When a component degrades, GlassHood tells a business user in plain language whether the cause is GlassHood's monitored system or an UPSTREAM platform/provider — so a non-SRE can instantly tell "this is the cloud platform's outage, our side is fine, nothing to escalate" from "this is ours." It delivers a stakeholder-facing impact statement (which service, business impact, expected scope/duration) through the channels stakeholders already watch (not a raw ticket payload), and after resolution retains a short plain-language incident RECORD (what happened, what changed) so the loop closes with a learning artifact, not just a mechanical auto-acknowledge.
- **Why:** The ticketing/incident-correlation/change-correlation/audit spine already exists, but three theme-residuals are absent and NOT owned by 005 or 006: there is no external provider-health "is it us?" context (a business user can't separate a platform outage from our fault); the only external route is the ticketing system of record, with no stakeholder-facing impact comms; and there is no post-incident record capturing lessons.
- **Acceptance:**
  - On degradation, a non-SRE is told plainly whether the cause is upstream-provider or our-side.
  - A stakeholder impact statement (service, business impact, expected scope/duration) reaches stakeholders through a channel they already watch — not as a raw ticket payload.
  - After resolution a short plain-language incident record (what happened, what changed) is retained.
- **State:** bidirectional incident sync, alert↔incident↔change correlation, and audit trails already exist; provider-context, stakeholder comms, and post-incident record do not.
- **Honesty constraint:** all of this is derived from and additive to the deterministic alert/correlation data — it never originates a figure or threshold. BOUNDARY: the per-alarm next-action / runbook carried AS A FIELD ON THE ALARM, and routing-enrichment-before-notify, are the in-flight REQ-GHA-006's job — NO next-action drafting happens in this REQ; 011 owns ONLY the provider-vs-us context, the stakeholder IMPACT comms, and the post-incident RECORD.
- **Status:** tracked, target-state.

## REQ-GHA-012: One consistent business-translation contract across every existing signal type (the moat, made consistent)
- **Goal:** The three signal types GlassHood surfaces to a user TODAY — an alert, a node-health change, and a topology/dependency change — each carry a CONSISTENT, deterministically-derived business-translation view stating in plain language: what changed, the likely business impact, and a role-tagged next action (who acts — business vs IT vs engineering — and what to check/do). No signal type is left with unreliable translation or with none. This makes the proven anomaly-classification pattern uniform across all three existing signal types.
- **Why:** This IS the moat — already partly real but INCONSISTENT. One path (the anomaly path) gets a rich deterministic classification (business impact + role-tagged business/IT/engineering actions, snapshotted per-alert), but the standard alert path produces no reliable structured business-translation, and topology-change/node-health events produce none at all. So the "act without an SRE translating" promise holds on one path and breaks on the others. Making the proven pattern uniform across the existing signal types is the genuinely additive, in-character gap.
- **Acceptance:**
  - Each of the three EXISTING signal types — alert, node-health change, topology/dependency change — carries the {what-changed, business-impact, role-tagged-next-action} view, derived deterministically.
  - No signal type falls back to no translation or to unreliable/ad-hoc translation.
  - Role-tagged actions are written portably (e.g. "review the perimeter/WAF logs"), naming no specific cloud product.
- **Cross-cutting note (NOT an acceptance gate):** future signal REQs (e.g. 007–011's new signals) should adopt this same translation contract; this REQ does not BLOCK on them — it closes once the three signal types above satisfy the contract.
- **State:** deterministic anomaly classification + AI analysis (score/summary/issues/recommendations) exist and are multi-tenant-isolated; the standard-alert and topology-change paths lack the contract.
- **Honesty constraint:** the impact statement and next-action are deterministically derived; the AI narrative is additive on top and never originates the impact or the action. Closed-loop AUTO-REMEDIATION / recover of a monitored client estate is deliberately OUT of scope — GlassHood is read-mostly and sits outside the validation path; the contract is translate-and-route-to-a-human, not automated change.
- **Status:** tracked, target-state.

## REQ-GHA-013: Distributed request tracing across services (SRE/engineering-grade — brand-name reference capability, NOT a business-user surface)
- **Goal:** GlassHood can follow a single request end-to-end as it traverses multiple services, recording the request's path and where time and failures occur — decomposed into per-service work and per-downstream-call detail — and correlate that trace with the same request's logs and metrics through a shared identifier. This is the full request-path signal (the fourth observability signal), of which REQ-GHA-007 surfaces only the business-legible slice.
- **Positioning (honest, by design):** This is SRE / engineering-grade infrastructure. Its value here is demonstrating distributed-tracing competence — a recognized, brand-name observability capability expected of a senior data/observability architect — NOT a business-user-facing surface and NOT part of the infra→business translation moat. The business-user payoff ("which dependency *link* is slow/failing") lives in REQ-GHA-007; this REQ is the deep tracing backend beneath it. Included deliberately as a reference-build showcase and labelled as such, so it is never mistaken for a moat surface.
- **Target-state acceptance criteria (portable; no cloud-product names):**
